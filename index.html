Section I

Introduction

About the film, its background, and our thesis

Released on June 12, 1998 to mark the centennial of Philippine independence, José Rizal is a historical epic directed by Marilou Diaz-Abaya and produced by Jeturian Productions and GMA Films. With a production budget of approximately ₱80 million, it was the most expensive Filipino film at the time (Diaz-Abaya, 1998).

Its release was an institutional act of cultural memory aligned with Republic Act 1425, which mandates the study of Rizal's life in Philippine schools to cultivate patriotism (Zaide & Zaide, 1999). The film uses Rizal's 1896 military trial as a framing device while exploring his life through structured flashbacks. Its most creative choice — including Ibarra and Simoun as living characters — cinematizes the internal tension between peaceful reform and armed revolution.

Our Thesis

José Rizal (1998) succeeds as an emotionally engaging work of cinema, but its tendency to idealize its subject, dramatize unverified events, and reduce complex figures limits its reliability as a historical document. Watched critically alongside primary sources, it remains a culturally significant educational artifact.

Summary of the Film

The story opens with Rizal's 1896 military trial before a Spanish tribunal. Through flashbacks, we trace his childhood in Calamba, education at the Ateneo and UST, studies abroad in Madrid, Paris, and Berlin — where he wrote Noli Me Tangere (1886) and El Filibusterismo (1891). His work in the Propaganda Movement, exile in Dapitan, and execution at Bagumbayan on December 30, 1896 close the story.

"Cinema, like literature, can be a vehicle for national consciousness — but only if it tells the truth."
Section II

Historical Accuracy & Creative Liberties

What the film got right — and where it took creative license

✅ What the Film Got Right
The broad arc of Rizal's life — studies at Ateneo and UST, travels to Europe, exile in Dapitan, trial, and execution — aligns with documented biography (Craig, 1913; Guerrero, 1963).
The film accurately depicts Dominican and Augustinian friars' control over land and governance, consistent with 19th-century conditions (Zaide & Zaide, 1999).
Rizal's composed and articulate self-defense during his military trial reflects primary accounts of the actual proceedings (Guerrero, 1963).
His collaboration with del Pilar and Lopez Jaena in the Propaganda Movement, through La Solidaridad, is faithfully represented.
The racial stratification between peninsulares, insulares, and indios is visually present throughout, grounding the film in colonial reality.
⚠️ Creative Liberties Taken
Ibarra and Simoun materialize as real characters. While effective in externalizing Rizal's moral conflict, this blurs biography with fiction for uninformed viewers.
Andres Bonifacio is shown as an emotional admirer rather than the independent strategist who founded the Katipunan — misrepresenting his historical significance (Ocampo, 1990).
Rizal's documented relationships with Leonor Rivera, Segunda Katigbak, and Josephine Bracken are minimized, contradicting the Epistolario Rizalino.
Josephine Bracken receives minimal screen time despite her controversial and historically significant role in Rizal's final years.
"A film that edits out a man's humanity in favor of his heroism teaches admiration, not understanding."
Section III

Scenes That Need to Be Debunked

Where the film dramatized or misrepresented history

Overdramatized
The Burning of the Rizal Family Home in Calamba
The film depicts Spanish authorities dramatically burning the Rizal family home as violent colonial retaliation. In reality, the family was forcibly evicted in 1890 through a legal tenancy dispute with the Dominican friars. There was no arson; the family was dispossessed through colonial courts (Guerrero, 1963).
Fact: It was a land dispossession, not arson. The dramatization distorts the legal nature of the injustice.
Historically Disputed
Rizal's Retraction and Spiritual Reconciliation
The film presents Rizal's retraction before execution as a sincere, settled moment. Historians remain divided on whether he genuinely signed any retraction — Ambeth Ocampo questions its authenticity and the conditions under which it was allegedly produced (Ocampo, 1990).
Fact: The retraction is one of PH history's most contested controversies. Staging it as resolved is a major historical overreach.
Disputed Location
Bonifacio's Cry at Balintawak
The film places the Katipunan's battle cry at "a small field in Balintawak." Historians debate whether this occurred at Balintawak or Pugad Lawin — and the film presents one version as definitive without acknowledging the controversy (Zaide & Zaide, 1999).
Fact: The exact location of the Cry remains officially unresolved. The film picks a side without evidence.
Overdramatized
Rizal Turning to Face the Rising Sun
The film's most iconic execution scene shows Rizal turning his body to face the sunrise so he would not be shot in the back like a traitor. While he reportedly made this request, historical accounts differ on whether Spanish authorities actually granted it (Craig, 1913).
Fact: Whether his final request was honored is historically uncertain. Powerful cinema — but not verified history.
"To study Rizal honestly is to resist the very myth-making his story so easily invites."
Section IV

Connection to Class Discussions

How the film connects to our actual lessons

The film serves as a visual companion to three major themes from our class discussions — on Republic Act 1425, on 19th-century Philippines as Rizal's historical context, and on the three phases of Rizal's life.

01
RA 1425
Republic Act 1425 & Why We Study Rizal
The film’s release in 1998 connects directly to RA 1425, which requires the study of Rizal’s life to promote patriotism (Zaide & Zaide, 1999). In class, we discussed an important question the film raises: does showing dramatized heroism really teach civic values, or does it just make people admire him without thinking critically? The movie’s idealized portrayal suggests the risk is real, which means analyzing it critically is actually part of following RA 1425.
02
19th-Century PH
The Philippines as Rizal's Context
The film also brings to life what we learned about colonial society: friar land control, the social divide between peninsulares and indios, the rise of the ilustrado class, the export crop economy, and the influence of European liberal ideas and Bourbon reforms (Guerrero, 1963). These scenes help us see the real conditions that shaped Rizal’s ideas and eventual push for reform.
03
Rizal's Life
Rizal's Life — Parts 1, 2 & 3
The film follows all three main phases of Rizal’s life. Part 1 shows his family and early education at the Ateneo. Part 2 covers his higher studies at UST and life abroad in the Propaganda Movement. Part 3 shows his exile in Dapitan, trial, and execution at Bagumbayan. Watching this gives a clear visual timeline that matches what we studied alongside the Epistolario Rizalino.
"The film is most valuable not when we accept it, but when we use it as a lens to question the history we already know."
Section V

Personal Reflections

What each of us genuinely felt watching the film

DJ
Group Leader
Ganub, Deo Joseph

Watching this film after spending time studying Rizal in class gave me a completely different perspective on who he was as a person. Growing up, I only ever saw him as a distant historical figure on coins and monuments, someone I was told to admire without really understanding why. The film changed that. Seeing his story play out visually made the stakes feel real in a way that no textbook had managed to do before. What I found most compelling was how the film portrayed the ideological conflict at the center of Rizal's life, specifically his belief in peaceful reform through education and writing at a time when many others were already demanding armed revolution. That tension, which we discussed at length in class, came across clearly in the narrative and helped me appreciate how genuinely difficult his position was. My main critique is that the film presents Rizal as almost impossibly composed and noble throughout, which makes him feel more like a symbol than a human being. I understand why that choice was made, given that the film was released during the centennial of Philippine independence and was clearly meant to inspire national pride. However, students who watch it without prior knowledge of Rizal's life may come away with a simplified and overly idealized impression of who he actually was. This is why I believe the film is best used as an entry point into studying Rizal rather than as a comprehensive account of his life and legacy (Ocampo, 1990).

TJ
Member
Almocera, Terence Johanne

Before watching this film, I understood Rizal mainly as a historical and political figure. What I did not fully appreciate was the personal weight of the choices he made. The film helped me see that more clearly. One of the scenes that affected me most was the courtroom sequence, where Rizal stood and defended himself before a military tribunal that had already decided his fate. The composure he showed in that moment, given everything he was facing, felt like it said more about his character than any biography could summarize in a paragraph. I also found the creative decision to include Ibarra and Simoun as active characters in the narrative to be one of the more thoughtful aspects of the film. Rather than simply referencing his novels, the film uses them to represent the competing sides of Rizal's thinking, the idealist who believed reform was possible and the revolutionary who had given up on that hope. This gave me a clearer sense of how deeply connected his writing was to his actual lived experience and his understanding of Philippine society. Where the film falls short, in my view, is in its refusal to show any of the contradictions or complexities that made Rizal a real and imperfect person. He is presented as consistently heroic, which is inspiring but ultimately a disservice to a more honest understanding of who he was (Guerrero, 1963).

SM
Member
Miraña, Sandy

The aspect of this film that affected me most deeply was not a single scene but the overall atmosphere it created around colonial life in the Philippines. Watching how Filipinos were treated by the Spanish colonial system, the control the friars had over land and livelihood, the racial discrimination embedded in everyday interactions, and the near impossibility of seeking justice made the conditions of that period feel viscerally real rather than like abstract historical facts. That emotional weight helped me understand, on a level that class readings alone had not fully achieved, why the push toward revolution eventually became unavoidable for so many people, even when figures like Rizal were actively urging restraint. At the same time, I found myself troubled by some of the film's dramatizations. Certain events, particularly those involving the Rizal family's displacement from Calamba, are presented in ways that do not accurately reflect the historical record (Guerrero, 1963). Learning afterward that the reality was a legal land dispossession rather than the dramatic scene shown in the film left me feeling somewhat misled. That said, I do not think the emotional truth the film is reaching for is wrong. Colonial injustice was real, and making audiences feel its weight is a legitimate artistic goal. The issue is when specific details are altered in ways that change the nature of what actually happened.

GP
Member
Pastorpide, Gian Paolo

Approaching this film from the perspective of someone interested in how stories are told, I was genuinely impressed by the craftsmanship on display. The cinematography by Rody Lacap creates a visual language that feels appropriate to the period without becoming stiff or overly formal. The production design is detailed and clearly well-researched, and the result is a world that feels inhabited rather than constructed for the camera. What stood out most to me on a storytelling level, however, was Joel Torre's performance in the dual role of Ibarra and Simoun. The challenge of embodying two characters who represent opposing sides of the same moral argument, and doing so within the same film, is considerable. Torre manages it by giving each figure a distinct emotional register. Ibarra carries a sense of idealism and genuine belief that things can improve, while Simoun conveys the exhaustion and bitterness of someone who has lost faith in that possibility. Watching those two performances alongside each other gave me a clearer and more intuitive understanding of the tension at the heart of Rizal's intellectual and personal life than I had developed from reading alone. It reinforced for me how valuable film can be as an educational medium when the filmmaking itself is thoughtful and deliberate.

PR
Member
Ramos, Paige

I went into this film with fairly low expectations, mostly because I was not sure how engaging a nearly three-hour historical epic from 1998 would be for me personally. I was wrong to be skeptical. Cesar Montano's portrayal of Rizal is one of the most quietly powerful performances I have seen in a Filipino film. He does not rely on dramatic outbursts or theatrical gestures to convey Rizal's emotional state. Instead, he communicates everything through restraint, and that understatedness actually makes the character feel far more authentic and compelling. The execution sequence in particular was difficult to watch in the best possible sense, because Montano played it with such stillness that it felt like you were witnessing something real. My most significant criticism of the film, however, is how little it develops the women in Rizal's life. Leonor Rivera and Josephine Bracken are both given minimal screen time and almost no character depth, despite the fact that primary sources such as the Epistolario Rizalino clearly indicate how significant and emotionally complex those relationships were to him. Their absence does not just represent a gap in the narrative. It actively diminishes the portrait of Rizal as a full human being and reinforces a version of him that is more legend than person.

SS
Member
Sy, Szenica

What lingered with me most after watching this film was not any specific scene but rather a broader discomfort with how the film positions itself in relation to historical truth. From the very beginning, every creative choice, from the framing of scenes to the musical score to how supporting characters respond to Rizal, seems designed to confirm a predetermined conclusion about who he was rather than to genuinely explore the complexities of his life. This approach becomes particularly problematic in how the film handles the question of Rizal's retraction before his execution. The scene is presented as a quiet and sincere moment of spiritual reconciliation, which gives the impression that this event is an established historical fact. In class, however, we studied how deeply contested this issue remains among historians, with significant scholarship questioning both the authenticity of the document and the circumstances under which it was allegedly produced (Ocampo, 1990). When a film stages something that disputed as settled, it does a disservice to audiences who may not have the background knowledge to recognize that the conversation is still open. That said, I want to be clear that I did find value in watching the film. It made Rizal feel like a real person in moments, which is genuinely difficult to achieve with a figure who has been so thoroughly mythologized.

LJ
Member
Villanueva, Lourdes Jed

One of the more interesting things our class discussed was how the mandate to study Rizal under Republic Act 1425 is not a neutral educational policy but a deliberate act of nation-building. The law exists because those in power decided that Rizal's story, presented in a particular way, serves an important civic function. Watching this film, I kept thinking about that idea, because the film itself is a very clear example of the same logic at work. It is grand, emotionally sweeping, and unapologetically patriotic, and it is structured so that by the end, you are supposed to admire Rizal and feel proud of what he represents. And honestly, it worked on me. I did feel those things. But I was also aware, in a way that I might not have been before taking this course, of what the film was choosing to leave out. The complicated and contested aspects of his life, his personal relationships, the disputed circumstances around his death, the ongoing historical debates about what he truly believed, are largely absent. The film does not ignore them out of carelessness. It omits them because including them would complicate the image it is trying to build. I do not think that makes the film bad. It makes it a specific kind of cultural artifact, one that tells us as much about how the Philippines wants to remember Rizal as it does about who Rizal actually was.

Critical Analysis

Cinematography & Educational Value

Filmmaking

Cinematographer Rody Lacap's sweeping visual language reinforces the historical backdrop effectively, while the production design — costumes, colonial architecture, and period interiors — reflects serious research and craft. These elements make 19th-century Philippine society feel inhabited rather than merely performed. Cesar Montano's restrained performance conveys Rizal's dignity without reducing him to sentimentality. Joel Torre's dual portrayal of Ibarra and Simoun is analytically rich: it cinematizes the ideological tension between peaceful reform and armed revolution that defines Rizal's intellectual legacy (Ocampo, 1990).

As an Educational Tool

The film carries significant limitations as a standalone educational resource. Its dramatizations of the Calamba land dispossession, the retraction scene, and Bonifacio's characterization require correction by primary and secondary sources. Used alongside Guerrero's The First Filipino (1963), Ocampo's Rizal Without the Overcoat (1990), and the Epistolario Rizalino, it becomes a productive starting point — not a conclusion — for understanding Rizal's life. It is most effective when it prompts questions rather than settles them.

Section VI

Conclusion & Film Rating

Our final take on the film

José Rizal (1998) is a cinematically significant and emotionally resonant tribute to the national hero, but its value as a historical text is limited by dramatization, selective omission, and a tendency to idealize its subject. As Guerrero (1963) and Ocampo (1990) both emphasize, the real Rizal was a far more contradictory and human figure than the film allows. The burning of the family home, the retraction scene, and the oversimplified portrayal of Bonifacio are specific examples where dramatic license overrides what the historical record actually shows. That said, the film does succeed in making the socio-political conditions of 19th-century Philippines feel real and vivid, and it captures the tension between reform and revolution in a way that is genuinely thought-provoking.

We recommend this film to any student of Philippine history, but we think it works best when it is watched alongside more critical and more honest sources. The version of Rizal that emerges from reading Guerrero, Ocampo, and the primary sources is actually more interesting than the one this film puts on screen, because that Rizal is complicated, contradictory, and fully human in a way that a centennial tribute cannot always afford to be.
